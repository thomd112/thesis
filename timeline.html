This is the timeline of Madhubani Art

◉ The paintings on the walls are known as Bhitti Chitra and the paintings on the floor are known as Aripana.

◉ Paintings were typically done on walls during holidays, religious celebrations, and other significant life events like marriage, birth, and the Upanayanam.

Madhubani paintings are traditionally done by women painters from the Mithila region.

They live in a small society, and every mother teaches her daughter the art of making Madhubani paintings.

Lenged of King Ranak: Originated in the Mithila region of India, dating back to the 8th or 7th century BCE -

Was traditionally drawn on walls of homes, known as bhitti chitra (wall paintings).

Key Characteristics: Passed down through families as a sacred tradition.Used for marriages, childbirth, and festivals like Chhath Puja.Painted with fingers, twigs, and natural dyes on mud walls.

Legend says that King Janak of Mithila commissioned the paintings to celebrate his daughter Sita's wedding to Prince Rama.

In 1934, British Colonial Officer William G. Archer discovered Madhubani paintings on the walls of homes in Bihar.

Archer wrote an article in Marg magazine comparing Madhubani paintings to the work of Picasso and Miro, which helped bring the art form to international attention.

Sita Devi presents her work of art to Prime Minister Indira Gandhi in New Delhi on September 8, 1969 (Image Credit: The Times Of India Group)

Madhubani painting received national attention when Sita Devi and Jagdamba Devi were awarded national awards by the President of India.

Madhubani painting was used in a conservation effort to protect trees in Bihar from deforestation.
